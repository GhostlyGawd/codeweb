Support

What sponsoring codeweb actually pays for

Everything local is free forever — the engine, all 27 MCP tools, the hooks, the report, the CLI, the self-hosted CI gate, every language, every fix. That is the contract, and it never changes. Money buys the two things an MIT license can't ship: the maintainer's time, and the benchmark spend behind every claim.

The fundable cost is real, and itemized

codeweb's whole brand is receipts — pre-registered hypotheses, published nulls, independent referees. Those receipts have a dollar cost: the frontier-agent A/B benchmarks are paid API tokens (the roadmap's later phases say "heavy multi-agent spend" out loud), and each new language needs trusted-wasm grammar provenance work. Sponsorship funds exactly that — the evidence machine, not a paywall.

Sponsor tiers are jobs, not amounts

  • $5/mo — keep CI and the bench corpus running.
  • $25/mo — vote on the next language (C/C++ is the loudest gap) + early access to new-language prereleases (≤30 days, time-shifted — never withheld, never fixes or security).
  • $250/mo (org) — name on the site and README, priority issue triage.

Running codeweb across an org? Enterprise support exists.

A throat to choke: SLA'd email support, private onboarding, architecture-rules setup (codeweb.rules.json + the fitness gate), and paid priority on language/feature work. $3–6k/yr flat, capped at a handful of customers — solo-maintainer attention is the scarce good, so it is priced, not given away in issues. Write to the address on the GitHub profile with "codeweb support" in the subject.

Anti-promises, stated plainly: no existing local feature will ever move behind a payment. No accounts, no telemetry, no license keys — ever. Asks appear only at success high points, computed from your local counters, at most monthly, and never in agent-facing responses or on error paths.
